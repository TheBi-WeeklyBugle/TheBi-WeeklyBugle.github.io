Minecraft has yet to engross me in the same way it did all those years ago. This phenomenon is nothing new; there are plenty of videos on YouTube dissecting Minecraft’s failures at living up to the nostalgia, but I had the chance to grow up alongside the game; I’m not another 20-something-year-old reminiscing about the “good old days.” Besides, this isn’t a video on how to fix Minecraft or why old Minecraft was better; it’s one critiquing the very way in which we understand it. Specifically, why Minecraft was never fun.

We only remember Minecraft so fondly because, honestly, we all sucked at it. Everyone remembers that first time they fought the Ender Dragon, the rush of dopamine from finding diamonds, or even the fear of zombies on their first night. The Ender Dragon used to be the absolute culmination of a long journey; now it’s beatable in 7 minutes and a bed cycle. Diamonds used to be something sought after; they were rare. Now they’re farmable within 5 minutes of starting a world. Nights used to be an actual concern and a challenge in and of themselves; now they're just a nuisance that requires all but one person to log off. Travelling for the sake of exploration used to be part of the journey; now the elytra’s efficiency has erased that part of the game entirely. Why spend all that time building a house when following a guide on YouTube would be so much faster? What would you do without the extra few hours to mine that fifth set of Netherite armor? Part of the fun came from discovering things, whether that be with friends or by yourself, and now everything’s one quick Google search away. I think we all remember that excitement from discovering something new and the anticipation for the next day when, in school, you could tell all your friends about it. For me, that was my discovery of infinite water. Minecraft has long stopped being a sandbox for us to create our own stories; it's about following the path everyone else takes. It’s fine to play a game just to beat it, but that’s just not what Minecraft is. It’s incredibly easy to maximize Minecraft, but in doing so, you end up minimizing the fun. The game was never about getting to the end, literally and figuratively, in the fastest way possible; it’s always been about creating. In that process, failure and inefficiency are inevitable. We tend to treat any setback as a waste of time. I’m also guilty of this, but by carrying this mindset, we lose sight of what really matters: fun. As long as you had fun doing it, or even if you just learned something, it was time well spent.

While my experience with the game might be far from universal [insert clip about the dragon or building], a lot of these modern “critiques” about Minecraft come from people that treat the game like it’s Ark. I understand that not everyone plays the game to “beat it,” but the reality is that the majority of people don’t play Minecraft the way it’s intended. Look at any of these critique videos; they always attack the progression of the game and never the actual nature of the sandbox because, I would argue, that aspect of the game has actually improved over time. You never see builders whine about the old Minecraft being better because, for them, every update brings new, fun mechanics to mess around with. The only people that make “two-week Minecraft phase” videos are the ones that just play for the survival aspect, hence the frequent complaint that there’s nothing to do in the game, but these videos perform amazingly. These nostalgia bait videos do so well for a simple reason: they reflect the opinion that you think it’s a skill issue; it absolutely is, but the reality of the situation is that not everyone cares enough to put in all the effort of creating something original. If you’re a part of the small minority that actually builds, Minecraft is a fantastic game, but that just isn’t the case for most people. In that sense, Minecraft will never be good because, as weird as it is to say, you’re playing Minecraft wrong. The game’s whole appeal lies in that ability to build whatever you want; it’s virtual Lego, and if you don’t like that aspect of it, Minecraft really is just a crappy, pixelated Ark. If you’re ok with that, that’s fine, just don’t expect Minecraft to be something it’s not. If you're excusing the game on the grounds that Minecraft isn’t about speedrunning the Ender Dragon, you're completely right, but it doesn’t discount the critiques from the majority of people. Both sides are completely correct in their evaluations of the game because they’re talking about completely different games; both opinions are wholly valid, and it’s hard to discount either.

I can’t title my video “Minecraft was never good" and then not talk about the multiplayer servers, at least not if I’m making an argument in good faith. Minecraft, and Roblox to a lesser extent, were my first experience with PvP (in a sense), and they really opened my eyes (so to speak). Bedwars, Skywars, anything Hy-Pixel really, was and still is awesome. Sadly, and I think everyone saw this critique coming, Minecraft sweating is on a whole other level. Everyone thinks they’re the next Technoblade or Dream, butterfly clicking their way to millions, and it’s completely ruined the point of any of these games. If I wanted to sweat my ass off, I would have played Fortnite; I play Minecraft to chill with friends and maybe play some fun mini-games, not to W-tap, block-hit, and god-bridge, all at 23 cps. There’s really not much to be said; I just don’t understand why people a play Minecraft PvP like it’s the Squid Games; just chill out, bro. 

Look, no matter how you slice it, we’ve outgrown Minecraft. It was never the game’s fault; although it has gotten worse, it was ours. All these problems are just symptoms of us getting older and a result of understanding the scarcity of time, but we can’t accept the change that comes with it. We cling to the joy that Minecraft brought us when we were younger, hence coming back every so often, but it’s time to let go. Minecraft was perfect for our juvenile selves because it met our demands at the time; it’s about time we accept those demands have changed. In 2016, Minecraft would still be disappointing to the current you because your standards have changed; no time machine can change that. It was perfect for you years past, and that’s okay. Let Minecraft go; cherish those memories, but know that the game will never live up to those expectations. So, please, move on.

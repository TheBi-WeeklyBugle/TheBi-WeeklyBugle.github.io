### Q1

Finance is about money. Traditionally, it’s about making lots of it. At MIT, it’s where the theoretical meets the practical, where it takes that uniquely quantitative approach. Yet, it’s really so much more. MIT focuses on solving those systematic problems inherent in finance, something I’ve yet to see in other schools. That focus on Financial Engineering and building a better world—that’s why I want to go to MIT. Through professors like Andrew Lo and classes like 15.482: Healthcare Finance, MIT produces those with the ability to create. With these opportunities, I can get ever closer to making that change.

### Q2

My YouTube channel has existed for 3 years. In that time I’ve amassed 28 subscribers, all of whom are friends. Yet, I still upload frequently.

The channel’s become a personal diary—I can cry about something stupid, complain about an anime, or talk about getting rejected from McDonald’s, all free from the world’s prying eyes. 

It’s the little things that make life sweet, yet they’re the first we forget. Barely catching the last train to school, a stranger’s encouraging nod as I run, the girl I like calling my broken glasses cute—those are the moments that call my channel home. Being able to share in the joys along with the minor grievances—that's what kept me coming back; it was never about views.

Once just a convenient place to rant, it’s become so much more. Even if I’ll never get back those hours spent talking to a camera, I couldn’t be happier.

### Q3

Long have the dreams of being an author faded; I’ve since put down my pen for good. Where I once found refuge in the sheets of my notebook, now there’s Google Sheets. That same excitement I felt when an idea clicked, the dopamine rush from a good sentence—it’s no longer there. What used to be a hobby has now become a tool, one I’ve begun to despise. 

On a whim, I applied for a film program my friend recommended. I wrote a quick story and submitted my application the night it was due, not really thinking too much of it; to my surprise, I got in. I never expected the program to change my life; the last thing I wanted to get into was the arts. Yet, seeing a story pan out in front of the camera, going from storyboard to finalized product, relit that same fire I used to get from writing. Those hours at the kitchen table, sketching comics without a care in the world—that’s what I missed. It was the desire to win contests and the pressure to land internships that I hated; I never hated writing, I hated the expectations.

If you told me I’d finally get around to writing a novella, having long given up on the idea, I would’ve called you crazy. Well, that’s what I’m doing right now. 

### Q4

My desk is a hoarder’s paradise; controllers and game systems litter the drawers. “I’ll use them someday,” I say, yet I never do. Still, I continue to keep all my electronics on life support, creating my own little ships of Theseus.

When a friend’s GPU broke, I offered to help fix it. I figured my experience with deconstructing handhelds would at least remotely transfer over; I couldn’t have been more wrong. Starting, I asked him a few questions, trying to figure out what was wrong. I diagnosed it like I would a handheld console, I tried looking for failure points or mechanical issues. Unsurprisingly, as you would expect from new parts, there were none. 

Already lost, I asked what he thought. We moved through the wires and to specific software interactions, yet none of it really got us any closer to solving the problem. There was no apparent damage on the outside of the GPU slot, nothing that raised a red flag. The computer was outright registering nothing, the GPU should have still been functional. Resorting to Google, we figured out the issue: the motherboard’s software wasn’t updated. 

Without my hardware intuition and his software know-how, I would never have guessed to check online for a possible update. Real collaboration, I learnt the hard way, was admitting when you’re lost. 

### Q5

Prior to attempting the creation of my PVC bow, I watched a 10-minute how-to video on YouTube and read a wikiHow article. It looked easy, so I bought the supplies; little did I know I needed a lot more than two six-inch pipes. 

That first attempt at the bow failed horribly—wrong heat settings on the gun, misaligned tin foil, and a burnt handle. Soon after, the crack spread across the handle and went past the point of no return, a land even duct tape can’t reach.

On the second go-around, I adapted. Where stepping on the PVC wasn’t enough, I got a makeshift clamp and manually did each limb. When the diagonal notches couldn’t hold the strings, I transitioned to admittedly uglier perpendicular ones, having to cut each limb short. I stopped trying to perfectly recreate that bow; instead, I focused on improving with every iteration. The bow ended up drawing sixty pounds.

After so much sustained stress to the PVC, the handle tore again. That week I learnt more than the mechanics of a bow and a recurve; I learnt to not chase that perfection. Half the problems started when trying to get those perfect notches; the other half came from overstressing the pipe. Instructions aren’t perfect. You can’t expect perfection on a first try; what matters is correcting those mistakes.

### Q1

I've never gone to bed hungry. My parents barely had enough to survive as kids. Eating meat to me is a matter of ethics; to them it’s of food. We’re fundamentally different; there’s no hope in reconciling the difference. Yet, we coexist. 

Every night, I feel like a stranger in my own home. The word “vegan” remains foreign; any attempt at an explanation has only resulted in dismissive, judgmental stares. Despite my best efforts, they won’t budge; meat is pushed onto the plate, eyes locked onto mine. To maintain the fragile stability, I oblige. Whenever they’re not looking, the meat goes back.

Backgrounds anchor perspectives in place; some differences are irreconcilable. I’m willing to compromise; I’m willing to understand, but I won’t be silenced.

Going into my senior year as class treasurer, the total amount of club funds requested ballooned to six hundred thousand—three times the previous. I was faced with an inevitable decision: prioritize the Goliaths, or the hundreds of Davids. Knowing what it felt like to be overlooked and understanding what it was like to be silenced, I just couldn’t overlook the runts. In the end, every club asking for less than two hundred got their full amounts.

To this day, I don’t know if I made the right choice. It was a decision the whole committee agreed to, yet it was difficult nonetheless. I chose to amplify the silence, to listen to those voices; it was the greatest good for the most people.

### Q2

Gazing through the train’s window after a long day, letting my mind wander—there’s no greater feeling in the world. A million questions race through my mind; I can’t answer one. 

The ability to satiate my chronic curiosity and the humbling realization that I know nothing—it’s something only philosophy can offer. Simply asking those questions, no matter how impossible they may be, is enough to fill a man’s heart. 

Philosophy has always been my first love; I want nothing more than to continue exploring the likes of Nietzsche and Freud. The tackling of unanswerable questions, the meddling in the deeply hypothetical—I live for it. Yet, at UNC, I want to do more than simply ask “why”; I want to figure out “how.” Long have I argued for what ought to be; now I want to explore what is. 

If I could do high school all over again, I would've spoken up sooner. It’s not until now I realize the importance of advocacy; why care what’s right when you’re not going to fight for it? UNC’s APPLES Service-Learning provides me with the opportunity to get involved with those communities that often can’t help themselves; opportunities like The Daily Tar Heel let me advocate for policies with real impacts, converting my passion for philosophy to real change.

I want to relive those moments on the subway on Chapel Hill’s free buses, continuing to ponder the unanswerable; real change won’t happen without someone to fight for it. UNC will be my battleground.

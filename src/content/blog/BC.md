### Q2

When we think of philosophers, we tend to imagine something akin to the greats—Socrates in the Athenian Agora, Nietzsche in his lonely room, and Dostoyevsky in a Siberian labour camp. When I think of philosophers, however, I can’t help but picture high schoolers around a lunch table, debating anything and everything. Of these many tables I’ve been at over the years, one stands out: a two-seater, with the stools too high, shared by one of my closest friends, Tanjimul Islam. 

Though we met as freshmen, we never had lunch together until our junior year. He always felt so distant, so different from me, as if separated by a wall. That year, our school had just rolled out 8th-period lunch to all the Muslim students so they could pray – that was where the questions began. Initially it started simple; I wondered why he was gone longer on Fridays, why there were dividers set up, and why the prayers sounded like songs. As the questions piled on and as I began to learn more, my interest only grew – that’s why I picked up a Quran.

When we weren’t debating why children got cancer, the validity of hadiths, or the meaning of jihad, we would conjure up the wildest disagreements. Many days were spent arguing for the love of the game; it’s just how we kept ourselves entertained. Sure, there would be stretches of months where we would relentlessly ponder the questions of the universe; the existence of free will was a particularly memorable one, but other times would see us arguing over whether a pizza should be eaten with a fork. The more we argued, the closer we got. Not every conversation had to be the most insightful, philosophically challenging debate; it became a way to kill time, to talk about anything.

We’ve lived completely different lives, yet we weren’t so different. Even oil and water can come together to create explosions under fire; Islam was the catalyst for that reaction. Those conversations exposed the fragility of the walls we artificially create; once you’re willing to open the gates, they don’t take long to fall. The willingness to discuss such controversial topics and the awareness to not step over lines brought us closer than Valorant ever could’ve; it made me realise those walls were but paper-mâché. We became lunch-table philosophers.

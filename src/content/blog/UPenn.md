### Q1

Dear Tina and Annette,

When you guys moved to Florida, I never got the chance to thank you. At the time I didn’t have the words to express my gratitude; thankfully, it’s not too late. 

You took me in and raised me as your own, taking care of me when my parents were off working. You guys were the ones that taught me English, ingraining in me a love for language; even today, I can still remember you reading Madeline. 

La dolce vita, the sweet life. The little things you guys did always made me feel welcome; all these years later, they’re what I remember most fondly. Scrabble, Go Fish, and those green marbles—they’ve all permanently etched themselves into my memory.

Tina, your gentle embrace was always there for me whenever I would run away from Annette, comforting me back into a consolable state.

Annette, as I’ve grown older, I’ve grown to appreciate how you could say it how it was. At the time it’s what I needed to hear; it’s not until now that I realize that.

I never got to thank you guys for everything, so for everything, thank you.

Love,
Stanley

### Q2

Gazing through the train’s window after a long day, letting my mind wander—there’s no greater feeling in the world. On that Wednesday night, there really was nothing more important than whether free will existed. 

Philosophy has always been my first love; I want nothing more than to continue exploring the likes of Nietzsche and Freud. The tackling of unanswerable questions, the meddling in the deeply hypothetical—I live for it. Yet, at Penn, I want to do more than simply ask “why”; I want to figure out “how.” Long have I argued for what ought to be; now I want to explore what is.

If I could do high school all over again, I would've spoken up sooner. It’s not until now I realize the importance of advocacy; why care what’s right when you’re not going to fight for it? Penn’s Netter Center offers a unique opportunity to experience the “how” that I seek to explore; I find its blend of academics and service work fascinating.

At Penn, I want to make it happen. I’m tired of waiting for change; I’m going to create it. To apply the philosophy I preach, to make that difference, it starts at Penn’s Civic House.

### Q3

Our financial systems are held together by duct tape and prayers. In my lifetime, Wirecard and FTX have been able to make away with billions in cash off their balance sheets; the system’s undeniably flawed. Despite the years of regulation added after Enron and the safeguards post-recession, these things still happen. The truth is, they’ll keep happening. In any game you expect cheaters; it’s not a question of how to stop them, it’s one of making it harder.

This year, the total amount of club funds requested ballooned to six hundred thousand—thrice the previous. Having just debuted a progressive, flat-rate deduction the year prior, it was immediately obvious: every club just requested more. The worst offender? Parliamentary debate, the student government vice president’s club. The formula got out.

Firsthand, I know what it’s like to be beat. At Wharton, I want to understand fraud. The Learning Lab seems like the best place to get a hands-on experience, to turn those theories into reality. Through Wharton’s extensive LGST curriculum, namely 2230: Securities Regulations, and with FNCE’s corporate governance, I want to learn the non-quantitative skills that’ll help me design systems where it takes more than compliance to outsmart the rules.

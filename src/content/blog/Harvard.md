### Q1

I've never gone to bed hungry. My parents barely had enough to survive as kids. Eating meat to me is a matter of ethics; to them it’s one of food. We’re so different; there’s no hope in reconciling the difference. Yet, we coexist. 

Every night, I feel like a stranger in my own home. The word “vegan” remains foreign; any attempt at an explanation has only resulted in dismissive, judgmental stares. Despite my best efforts, they won’t budge; meat is pushed onto the plate, eyes locked onto mine. To maintain the fragile stability, I oblige. Whenever they’re not looking, the meat goes back.

Backgrounds anchor perspectives in place; some differences are irreconcilable. I’m willing to compromise, I’m willing to understand, but I won’t be silenced. At Columbia, I won’t be afraid to be loud. To challenge the status quo, to fight for those otherwise unheard—that is what I bring.

### Q2

I’m not a music person, yet I own nearly every Taylor Swift album. When “The Life of a Showgirl” first came out, a friend and I listened through it together; over its 40-minute run time, he lodged nearly 40 complaints. He complained non-stop about the sound; I wouldn’t drop the storytelling. Taylor Swift, to me, is a writer; to my friend, the songs were art, the melodies themselves stories told.

We stopped trying to convince one another. Instead, we took turns listening to it again: one track for the story, the next for the sound. Lost, I asked for directions. He showed me through his playlist, and I gave every artist a shot, learning to listen. Later, I talked him through the lyrics.

Neither of our opinions changed, yet our disagreement resolved. Engagement isn’t about winning; it’s about learning how someone hears the world.


### Q3

My YouTube channel’s existed for 3 years. In that time I’ve amassed 28 subscribers, all of whom friends. Yet, I still upload frequently—I couldn’t be more content. The channel’s become a personal diary of sorts—I can cry about something stupid, complain about an anime, or talk about getting rejected from McDonalds, all free from the world’s prying eyes. It was the one place I could completely drop the facade, looking as dumb as I wanted—my own little bubble. 

One day, it suddenly popped. My classmates had found out about the videos; for all I knew, my social life was over. Yet, no one really cared; if anything, it was cute. It was all in my mind this whole time.

I assumed that being seen meant being judged, that authenticity only existed in isolation. However, this experience taught me that vulnerability wasn’t embarrassing—shame is something we create in our own heads. 

### Q4

The curiosity that compels me to question our reality at lunch, the insatiable hunger that keeps me debating between bites—that’s what keeps me asking why.

I’m not interested in just pricing bonds; I want to ask the things that matter. The nature of the human condition and the ethics of a free market are questions uniquely explored in Philosophy and Economics (EC 2050 / HLS / HKS). An education that grounds the quantitative field of economics in the great works of the past—that’s something only Harvard could offer.

Outside the classroom, I’m excited to join the HEA. I admire the organization’s approach to Effective Altruism and relate to it deeply, having previously explored Consequentialism. The focus on intellectual exploration is one of its kind, one only found there.

At Harvard, I hope to bring those lunchroom debates to a new stage, applying my "why" to a new set of questions.	

### Q5

So we’re roommates. I’m excited for us to meet, but you should probably know some stuff before we get into things. No, I’m not a serial killer. 

I wouldn’t blame you if you would have assumed so; you might occasionally hear the floors creak at three in the morning. The actual reason’s because I like to make instant ramen when it’s way past midnight—I have no idea why. On the bright side, feel free to help yourself to the ramen. 

When we’re actually both awake, you can expect to decipher lots of analogies. I hope you’re caught up on football; you’ll need it. Depending on the day you might also need an extensive knowledge on philosophical thought experiments; those are also frequent suspects. 

Lastly, while I can be sarcastic at times, I know how to be serious. If you ever need me for anything, I’ll be there for you.

### Q1

I've never gone to bed hungry. My parents barely had enough to survive as kids. Eating meat, to me, is a matter of ethics; to them, it’s one of food. We’re so fundamentally different; there’s no hope in reconciling the differences. Yet we coexist.

Every night, I feel like a stranger in my own home. The word “vegan” remains foreign; any attempt at an explanation has only resulted in dismissive, judgmental stares. Despite my best efforts, they won’t budge; meat is pushed onto the plate, eyes locked onto mine. To maintain the fragile stability, I oblige. Whenever they’re not looking, the meat goes back.

Backgrounds anchor perspectives in place; some differences are irreconcilable. I’m willing to compromise, and I’m willing to understand, but I won’t be silenced. I understand what it’s like to be ignored; I refuse to stand idly by as it happens to others. 

This year, the total amount of club funds requested ballooned to six hundred thousand—three times the previous. I was faced with one of two decisions: take an equal cut, in this case, sixty-six percent from everyone’s final allocatable amount, or play favorites. In the end, I couldn’t take two-thirds from a club that requested a mere hundred. The biggest clubs, the ones that requested thousands of dollars, were the ones that saw the steepest cuts. Requests that initially totaled thirty thousand were cut down to ten; every request under two hundred was left untouched.

Of course I got pushback, and a substantial amount at that, but I sat down and listened. I understood what it was like being told no without a reason—I didn’t owe them money, but the least I could do was have a conversation. I wasn’t going to be the reason someone felt silenced—I understood the pain all too well.

### Q2

Gazing through the train’s window after a long day, letting my mind wander—there’s no greater feeling in the world. On that Wednesday night, there really was nothing more important than whether free will existed. 

Philosophy has always been my first love; I want nothing more than to continue exploring the likes of Nietzsche and Freud. The tackling of unanswerable questions, the meddling in the deeply hypothetical—I live for it. Yet, at Michigan, I want to do more than simply ask “why”; I want to figure out “how.” Long have I argued for what ought to be; now I want to explore what is. 

To make real change I’m going to need to do a lot more than think. Through Michigan’s aptly named REAL program, I can dip my feet into the waters. Rarely do you find a program that brings the talk outside just the classroom, opening real-world opportunities and chances to practice what you learn. Getting that hands-on experience and seeing how theory plays out in practice—that’s more valuable than anything you can learn in a classroom. Through courses like the Social Venture Fund (ES 703) taught by Prof. Gautam Kaul, I hope to expand my understanding of business. More than how to make a profitable investment, the class's focus on creating a social surplus resonates with me—it’s how I see business. All the money in the world would be nice, but isn’t the whole point the greatest good for the most people?

Business is how I’ll change the world, but I’ll never forget where I come from. Ross’ flexibility with their BBA and the interdisciplinary culture make pursuing my passion in philosophy really doable. The ability to take half my classes outside purely for business is one very unique to Michigan, and it’ll give me that comprehensive education that I’m looking for in college; if I were looking for job training, I would have gone to trade school. I value the opportunity to talk to professors like Elizabeth Anderson and hear her theories on the intersection between ethics and economics while still being able to explore the practical applications of business. Being able to question the frameworks that we accept as the norm, to question the "why" along with the "how"—that is why I value a Ross education. 

At Michigan, I plan to apply to LSA’s PPE program. Using that cross-school flexibility that Ross provides with their BBA program, I want to pursue that passion for philosophy and take a unique spin on my approach to business. As I’m considering impact consulting as a future career, this choice also provides a nice degree of depth to my understanding of the finance world, and it would give me the skills to answer the questions that matter. Not only will I be able to perform in the existing frameworks, but I’ll also have the ability to change them. That’s how I plan to answer both what is and what ought to be.

The same curiosity that kept me awake and off my phone on those train rides—that’s why I want to go to Ross. Seldom are you going to find a school that makes it so easy to explore my two passions equally while still giving me the opportunity to make real-world changes. That’s why I’m ready to go blue.

### Q3

To paint a 28 mm miniature, it takes 8 brushes. Priming, basing, highlighting, and detailing all require one; the other 4 are there because the first 4 are never enough. That’s not even mentioning the junk brushes that get thrown out after using an impossible-to-wash-out paint or the ones that just get ruined with time. Because of the ridiculously expensive hobby that is Warhammer, the costs associated with the paintbrushes might seem minuscule. Yet, to a high schooler trying to sustain the crumbs he made over summer break, they add up. It’s one thing to justify buying a $45 miniature; try 8 brushes that range anywhere from $5 to $20 each. 

The paintbrush has been used since time immemorial. For such a lengthy and storied history, it’s rarely seen much change. Proposing any substantial difference might seem like reinventing the wheel, and it’s a fair concern, but a lack of change isn’t necessarily a sign of perfection. Things normalized get frozen in time—familiarity breeds contempt. Far too long has it been acceptable to buy eight paintbrushes on top of an army costing $170—that’s why I’m proposing a modular paintbrush. Pens have long since gone modular; I don’t see why paintbrushes should be any different. 

Not unlike a modular pen, the brush will let you swap its head. Instead of selling entire brushes and wasting a tremendous amount of wood, all my company would need to do is sell the chassis once and then have a customer for life. Not only would this condense the number of paintbrushes you need to buy, but you could also sell the tips separately. As brush heads often go bad with time or are thrown away after a particularly coarse paint, this would also provide a steady stream of revenue. In a market full of consumers very willing to pay for subscriptions, and with the success of Gillette for generations, the razor and blade model has proved itself to be a successful one. The brushes can be placed on a platform with a conical screw; the chassis will have screw indents where the two connect. Thus, this allows for an easy-to-assemble brush without having the potential issues that come with magnets.

Despite how niche of a market this may be, I believe there’s tremendous earning potential. Warhammer has been around since my parents were teenagers; the demand has firmly established itself. Games Workshop, the company that owns the exclusive rights to the IP, has proved itself incredibly profitable, and plastic modeling, the broader hobby, has seen continued growth with the success of Gunpla and Bandai in the West. In a market where consumers can justify spending nearly fifty dollars on a model they have to paint and assemble themselves, you can imagine how large the margins are. 

In a society dominated by brand loyalty, see the Marlboro Man, it’s almost a guarantee that the razor-and-blade business model would work. Especially with the convenience provided, the loyalty of Warhammer fans, and a potential deal with Games Workshop on the table, the product's success is all but guaranteed. There’s a low upfront cost with the chassis, which could be sold at a loss similar to razors, but a high lifetime value for both the consumer and the company. The frustration with spending so much on disposable brushes: I see that as an opportunity. The cost of brushes ultimately isn’t absurd, but it’s an inconvenience—one that no one has bothered to solve. Modular brushes are the answer.

Over the summer, I applied to this summer media intensive on a whim. I’d always had a vague interest in film, and it seemed like an interesting opportunity; after all, I only had so much time until I had to pay taxes. I really didn’t even expect to get in; I had no background in film and was already pretty set on business school by that point, so imagine my surprise at the acceptance email. After finishing the program, I was signed on for another year as a media fellow—that’s when I made my first movie solo. 

The attached artifact is a website link containing that first movie I made; it’s the culmination of everything I learned about film. The little narration about groundhogs is my attempt at an allegory of the cave—it’s the essence of my passion for writing and philosophy. It’s an experience I’ll never forget—seeing such a big project through from beginning to end. To be able to turn a story into a film truly unique to me is an opportunity I could have never imagined I would have; it’s a feeling I haven’t found elsewhere. 

That whole experience of shooting taught me a lot about myself. I’ve long since known I can be a perfectionist; I didn’t know how bad it could be. Every shot was reshot multiple times, and every little thing was meticulously scrutinized. No matter how much time I spent, though, mistakes were made. My inability to stop screwing up—that was the biggest lesson. Mistakes were going to be made, and I had to make the best of them; I learned to accept less than perfect.

“My Little Pony” is one of the best series of all time. Honestly, I would say the writing is on par with the likes of “Avatar: The Last Airbender" and other great shows. Outrageous claim, I know, but MLP manages to make the world seem like a slightly better place. The characters are wonderfully written, and after three full watch-throughs, I’m comfortable saying I doubt anything will replicate its success again. It's kind of crazy saying that as a sixteen-year-old guy, but this is the world we live in. That being said, it’s great that I can openly adore this show without fearing too much hassle. Why? Largely, it’s because of media like MLP and their role in shaping my generation. FiM transcends beyond its role as a children’s cartoon—it's taught a generation of kids what it means to love (Hook's definition), dream, and be a human (pony?). I could go on about this forever, but for the sake of everyone’s time, I won’t. Today, we’ll be focusing on one episode in particular: Slice of Life. 

Celebrating one hundred episodes of the show, Slice of Life somehow manages to be the perfect encapsulation of what makes MLP great to me. It’s a wholesome, cute story about friends, and if you look deeper, it’s both really funny and clever. Alright, fangirling aside, let’s start with probably my favorite part of the show, the queer representation. Kinda crazy to say, I know.

The lasting cultural impact of FiM can largely be attributed to its, at the time, revolutionary stance on homosexuality. The original thematic premise of the show is its subtitle: Friendship is Magic. This, in practice, translated to a love that transcends all barriers. Even today, this show represents a mainstay in the LGBT community. This show goes out of its way to emphasize global inclusivity, and that’s partially why this audience developed initially. Because of Equestria's open and inviting setting, the show enables the viewer to imagine themselves within the world. That’s also why there are so many OCs and furries in the fandom. Let's keep in mind this show came out in 2010, so a lot of things deemed normal to us today that are PC were absolutely radical back in the day. One of the lead characters, Rainbow Dash, is quite literally a walking rainbow. It doesn’t get more overtly gay than that. Even the original color scheme of the title, purple, pink, and white, is the same on the lesbian flag. Is that a coincidence? Maybe. Ok, background aside, let’s talk about who we’re here for today: LyraBon.

"Binary" is a weird word. Deriving its roots, the prefix "bi-" directly implies two. Now, we know that gender is a spectrum. Over the years, this word has transformed into representing opposites on the rainbow. Literally, as in two-sided, and figuratively, as in gay. True, unconditional love, however, is one free of binaries, manufactured gender norms, or even heterosexual bias. The beauty of LyraBon, besides its cuteness, is that it’s universal. We’ll get back to them later. In a time where homosexuality was really controversial, by proxy of being so radical, it was an amazing feat to portray a queer relationship as relatable. After all, the first step of acceptance is to understand. Real quick, before we start the deep dive, MLP’s depictions of friendship, relationships, and life are just so… real. They’re what makes this show truly amazing. Everyone, even with their closest friends, is going to argue. MLP doesn’t skip over this fact; instead, it emphasizes it. The show doesn’t shy away from any of these complex topics, even with an audience largely composed of kids, and that’s honestly incredibly impressive. Hell, it even subconsciously helped me to understand and accept same-sex relations at a time in your life where you’re not exactly the most open-minded. I'm not going to lie, I’ve always been a hardcore AppleDash shipper. Its depictions of non-heteronormativity were genuine, and by not specifically making a marvel out of anyone gay, they normalized and authenticated it. The key is, and take notes, Disney, they didn’t virtue signal to me. 

The title itself, Slice of Life, is incredibly emblematic of the episode’s theme: everybody (pony) is the star of their own story. Hell, those are Mayor Mare’s words, word for word, bar for bar. This thematic premise is exactly why this episode is so beloved and, more importantly, so effective under critical analysis. Its focus on individuality is refreshing and, honestly, really damn interesting. Yes, this is symbolic of Marxist tenets of the conflict between the state and individual, but for this, I think it’s more interesting when considering the intended audience. First, the obvious fact: this show is meant for children. Usually, I blow up when anything is forcefully told, but considering the age demographic, it makes sense. Children have the natural tendency to form a pack mentality, and thus, individuality is frowned upon in favor of being “normal.” Additionally, it’s particularly important considering the episode is a celebration of the show and thus should be easily understood by anybody. Regardless, it’s a sweet way to thank both the viewer and remind them that yes, their quirks make them special. 

When I mentioned children’s natural tendencies to collectivize, like Lord of the Flies, I also noticed that this inherent measure is a societal construct. Fundamentally, humans are selfish. We group up because of strength in numbers. But why do we demonize individuality? Largely, it’s because of capitalism. While, yes, capitalism DOES inherently reward individual prioritization monetarily, its problems arise in factories and mass industrialization. I think everyone can agree there’s not much room for self-expression in a factory line. Why are we taught to fit in when we’re young? Because it’s easier to conform and settle than aim for the stars. Also, society would much rather have a mindless laborer, and fundamentally, that’s one of the problems with our education system. That’s a whole other argument, though. In our society, in which everything is commodified, human lives are too. We’ve gone past the slavery days, but the soul of it still remains. Why did we enslave people? Not because they were black, and frankly, whoever argues that is stupid; it’s because it’s optimal for profits. At this point, we’re so far detached from My Little Pony, so to make it make sense, let’s talk about how these concepts relate to and reflect the show’s utopian view of society. 

One of the funniest parts of the episode, Gummy’s monologue on the futility of pony life, also works as a critique on pony society. Let me play that clip for you first. Fundamentally, pony society is utopian because of the cutie marks. There is no class division, hence no bourgeoisie or proletariat, due to every pony’s job being tailored to them. Money and class are never a problem, regardless of occupation, and social divides are non-existent. You see princesses, farmers, and everything in between interacting; it reinforces the show’s message of blind love, and hey, it’s wholesome as hell. This can also further appeal to adults disillusioned with capitalism and small, naive children. This emphasis on the cutie mark, the glue that holds the utopia together, is critiqued by Gummy. If you remember, he says, “And what of the poor gator? Flank forever blank, destined to an existential swim down the river of life to... an unknowable destiny?” As Gummy is an alligator, obviously, and Equestria revolves around cutie marks, he is understandably lost and thus "destined to an existential swim down the river of life.” Without a cutie mark—and by proxy, a certain future—he's lost through the river of life. All this considered, I think it’s fair to make the claim that a utopia, or ultimate fulfillment, hinges on a meaning to life. Thus, a utopia is a world in which life has meaning.

In a sense, this episode is the amalgamation of what makes this show feel special and why it’s adored all these years later. First, let’s start with Steven Magnet. He’s getting a spa treatment, not exactly the most masculine thing, and… he talks like this: [INSERT SPA CLIP]. He presents a complete reversal of masculine roles and the performativity of gender; it’s a complete departure from the macho sea serpent we imagine in favor of an empathetic, flamboyant friend. 

Adding onto MLP’s challenging of societal norms, BonBon, or [SPECIAL AGENT SWEETIE DROPS], is a complete 180 on everything you expect. In common media, you usually don’t see women portrayed as badass or secretive—the only female secret agent I can think of is Black Widow, but she’s hypersexualized and more of a femme fatale. So, imagine my surprise when some random side character announces her status as an undercover agent. When considering the fact that she’s lesbian, a label that doesn’t necessarily associate itself with the dangers of being a spy, it’s a jarring juxtaposition of traditional roles in media and a clever one in making a character memorable. More importantly, she’s the perfect person to represent someone queer. There’s a popular misconception that anyone non-heteronormative is obviously different and thus can be easily spotted out. A “gaydar” if you would. This generally stems from society’s complete normalization of traditional relationships. Considering her alter ego, it makes it all the more suiting as someone gay, who you’d assume sticks out, is as secretive as a spy. Also, BonBon revealing her dual identity can also be interpreted as a metaphor for coming out as gay. It’s not totally unbelievable to assume that people, like Lyra, would feel betrayed or hurt. After all, their whole perception of you is built off the false assumption that you’re straight. Coming out of the closet is jarring—even if people are accepting. If I were to propose a new social myth, it would be along the lines of “everyone sticks out, just in their own little ways.” After all, the reason why people believe they possess a “gaydar” is because of external personality traits. Oftentimes, for queer people, this can mean stereotypes that conflict with their biological sex. I think it’s obvious, but not every gay person is overly flamboyant or tomboy-ish. Besides all that, the actual depiction of their relationship is really genuine, and showing their argument play out was a great way to one, depict healthy communication, and two, make homosexual relations relatable. They go through the highs and lows of a typical relationship, straight or gay, and they show how queer relationships aren’t, actually, that different. They’re also super damn cute and wholesome, and it’s impossible not to root for them. 

Ok, we’re finally in the home stretch. And I don’t know if I’ve emphasized this enough, but this show is really damn cute. In order to talk about lenses and theories, we need to first firmly establish the overarching theme: I’ve danced around it, but MLP is fundamentally a story about love. When fully dissected and viewed through the queer and reader response lenses, you can make out MLP as a critique of social norms and arbitrary class divisions. At its roots, the show is, once again, about love and its limitlessness. It’s important to remember, however, that its thematic premises and critiques—those being love and status—are not necessarily mutually exclusive. Personally, I think the whole point of the series was to show that, regardless of societal constructs, love can prevail. The depiction of queer relationships and the interaction between the rich and poor all go towards constructing a utopia in which status isn’t linked with material wealth. At the very end of the episode, we can see firsthand ponies of all different colors, shapes, and races come together under one roof to celebrate the wedding of Frankie and Matilda. Now, if you were to combine all three lenses, you would get something akin to the subtitle of the show: Friendship is Magic. Specifically, friendship has the ability to tear down any pre-established barriers and is the first step to universal acceptance. Friendship can, and should, be blind. Generic, I know, but it’s really damn suitable.

If anything, I don’t think I’m the target demographic for this show. I’m a straight dude, and this show’s for the empowerment of young girls. That being said, and I don’t think I’m in the large minority, I can still see myself somewhat reflected. Every character is so diverse yet so grounded, and they all have their emotional beats. Eventually, the gender of the characters fades away and you're left with something so inherently human. I think that’s the beauty of MLP.

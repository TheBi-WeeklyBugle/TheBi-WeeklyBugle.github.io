History doesn’t care about losers. If you were to ask an American about Sputnik’s legacy, chances are they wouldn’t be able to give you an answer. Hell, they wouldn’t even know what a “Sputnik” is. Of course, that’s largely just American ignorance with a hint of Cold War education. Now, if you were to ask of Apollo 11, I really doubt many in the States would even hesitate in providing an answer. If not Apollo, they’ll at least know of the first man to walk on the moon: Neil Armstrong. Even if you were to conquer all of Europe, in a surge of glory the French would never see again, a singular loss would have banished you to Elba. If that sounds familiar, Napoleon met that fate after being forced to abdicate his throne. Of course, history tells us he had one last fight within him. The loss at Waterloo, however, just resulted in his banishment to another island; this time, it was St. Helena. We got a really good movie out of it, though. Besides sealing his fate, Waterloo cemented his place in history: a glorious but short-lived conqueror who, ultimately, was a loser. So, what do all these things have in common? Besides all finishing a little short (just like me), they demonstrate one fundamental thing that makes us human: the unrelenting drive for more. Let’s keep that in mind moving forward. With all that said, let’s actually talk about who we're all here for today: Sisyphus, arguably history’s biggest loser. 

Sisyphus was the original founder of Corinth (what was once supposedly named Ephyra), and he was known as avaricious, deceitful, and cruel, often killing guests within his palace. This angered Zeus, and, as we’ll discover later on, this will damn him to an eternity in hell. If you’re thinking, “Oh, Zeus didn’t like the arbitrary homicide," then you would be gravely, gravely mistaken. Let’s not forget, this is the same man that rapes women just because. Matter of fact, this fury came from the fact that this broke Greece’s "guest obligations," which fell under Zeus’ domain. So, was this enough to condemn him to a fate quite literally worse than death? Of course not. This background section is mostly irrelevant to my metaphor, and thus, everything is going to be left extremely broad until we arrive at the Underworld; this is just to catch you up to speed. Also, one, I think it’s interesting, and two, I need that ad revenue. From where we left off, Sisyphus once again pisses Zeus off: this time, however, he snitched on the whereabouts of some nymph that the river god “Asopus” was searching for. This obviously angers the god, and ultimately, Sisyphus ends up chained in Tartarus. He ends up tricking Thanatos, the god of death, into the chains used to trap him—in some versions, this is Hades—and this causes some unforeseen consequences. By the way, while writing this script, I realized Thanatos and Thanos—the Marvel supervillain—sounded awfully familiar. Upon further research—and by that, I mean consulting the first Google search result (in this case, a Quora)—the Marvel baddie’s (not like that) name does actually draw inspiration from this obscure Greek god. When Sisyphus eventually dies—I believe his death was because he was forced back into Thanatos’ hands—he decides it would be a great idea to trick Persephone into releasing him—the how varies and I’m too lazy to describe it. As punishment for evading death twice, and after being dragged back into Tartarus again by Hermes, he’s condemned to an eternity pushing a boulder up a hill. Famously, every time Sisyphus nears the top, the boulder rolls back down. The punishment is supposed to symbolize Sisyphus’ hubristic belief in his cleverness (according to Wikipedia) backfiring—as he believed he was smarter than Zeus himself—but the simplest answer is the right one: a life of repetitive, meaningless work is worse than death itself. There’s probably a connection you can draw to that and a 9-5, but I’ll let you fight that fight.

Now, that was just my headcanon for “why” and not necessarily “what.” In actuality, one can hold a million different interpretations of the story, and that’s the beauty of the medium. Some have seen it as a story of hope, the neverending pursuit of goals, or the futility of human life. For the sake of my story, we’ll be looking at it, incredibly ironically, from the perspective of 1st-century BC philosopher Lucretius Carus. In his “De rerum natura”—in English, “On the Nature of Things”—he likens the story of Sisyphus to politicians and their constant defeat in the eternal struggle for power. To put it simply, this pointless chase of status is, in his words, an “empty thing” and thus Sisyphean inherently in nature. As a quick side note, one of my favorite interpretations of the myth is the likening of futility to hope. A really common lens people use when it comes to this is one of nihilism—the pushing of the boulder being a metaphor for the eternal pointlessness of human life. While this is a valid, if not great, reading of events, I think it’s much more interesting when examining the relationship between hope and the futility of our lives. Especially when you look at the scale of the universe—and by proxy, the concept of “infinity”—humanities' perseverance is frankly amazing. 

Regardless of your chosen interpretation, Sisyphus’ story is universal. After all, he serves as the embodiment for the most fundamentally human thing: struggle. That being said, you may be asking, "What does this have to do with anything?” The first video, my best foot forward, is just copy and paste from Wikipedia? Well, as the title suggests, this is an introduction to my channel. You’ll see why in just a second. Before that, let me just state for the record that while everything so far has been a Wikipedia page overly condensed, everything from this point on will be my personal insight and my story. 

The myth of Sisyphus, to me, is one about the strength of hope. The definition of insanity is doing the same thing over and over again while expecting different results (according to Albert Einstein?). That isn’t the actual definition, but for the sake of this argument, we’re going to assume it is. In a sense, hope is the belief that things will get better regardless of circumstance. Thus, hope is insanity. To persevere is to push onwards with nothing but hope, and hope is that willingness to push. The reason why we find his story so fascinating, all these years later, is that—fundamentally—it's human. We, as a collective whole, are Sisyphus. If the dots have not connected, hope, struggle, and futility are the definitions of life. Even in the face of human life’s hopelessness, we still continue to push. Ultimately, everything we do is futile. So why? [INSERT JFK]. What’s the alternative? Giving up. For me, using this established definition of life, I’ve truly lived for the first time.

If you recall Lucretius’ interpretation of Sisyphus, it’s one of political failure. Back when I covered him, I called his take ironic. As to why, this whole project of mine is because of coming short in school politics. Feeling like I wasted a year of my life, I decided to put my spare time to use. Turns out, I grossly (and dangerously) overestimated my free time, but that's beside the point. Being absolutely lost, as every open door closed, I decided to pick up my pen and write. By the end of the hour, I drafted this. From the writing of this script to the finished production, I’ve learned how to photoshop, edit, and function on insane sleep deprivation. Look, I know the way I write isn’t everyone’s cup of tea. But, if you give me some time and your support, I really do think I have it in me, though. Regardless, I’ll give it my 110%, and you have my word.

Moving away from Sisyphus, let’s talk about another Greek myth. I promise this’ll make sense. I think everyone knows his story, but I have some time to buy, so let’s talk about the man who flew too close to the sun: Icarus. As the story goes, Icarus and his father were trapped in the labyrinth—the same one that the Minotaur was trapped in—and they made wax wings to escape. As to why they’re in the dungeon, it’s too long of a story, and this is all besides the point. Famously, once they make it out of their prison, Icarus gets cocky with his newly found freedom, and eventually, he flies too close to the sun. He falls into the Icarian sea, hence its name, and dies. Besides being a metaphor for excessive ambition and, figuratively, “flying too high,” his story gives a very clear warning: the Icarus Complex. Funny enough, while researching this script, I stumbled upon this phenomenon. I think it’s really fitting and sadly, really true. In simple terms, it’s when an incompetent individual, in an attempt to overcompensate, has expectations set too high. In this case, and I think it’s pretty obvious, I have no experience, I’m a subpar writer, and I’m way too young. If anything, though, the one thing in the world I have is time. With some luck, maybe this will work.

Jumping off that idea, and I’m sorry this script is so disorganized, I’m literally sacrificing my health for this. Screw anything that requires time—an example being having a girlfriend—I don’t even have the energy to study. That’s not even mentioning the lost sleep, less time spent with friends, and constant stress. I literally starve myself, as I don’t have time to eat lunch at school to work on this. In a sense, I’m wagering my life for the most miniscule chance at something. The alternative? Sitting on my ass, I’d rather die than regret wasting my time. Icarus’ mistake wasn’t flying too close; it was making his wings out of wax. Who knows, maybe I’ll rot in Tartarus pushing a boulder eternally, or maybe I’ll reach the stars. Only time will tell. 

If all of this doesn’t make sense yet, that’s fine. I understand that I can be vague in my writing. In the simplest way possible, this is an introduction to me and my channel. I thought relating my story to Sisyphus's would be an interesting way to frame my thought process. The Icarus stuff is my nihilistic side talking (the sensible one), but part of me wants to believe. Everything Sisyphus embodies to me—hope, futility, and perseverance—was attempted to be portrayed somewhere through my writing. At the end of the day, I’m doing this because I have hope. I choose to believe—not because it’s smart, but because it’s better than giving up. Right? Even if it costs me my sanity, I’ll write, and hey, maybe this’ll work out.

OK, one last conclusion paragraph. The world always has a funny way of working. Oftentimes, with failure, people say everything will work itself out. “God’s Plan,” if you will. While I’ve heard of countless people beating all odds, history forgets the losers. What does this mean for our old friend Sisyphus? In Greek mythology, and in popular media, Sisyphus has been largely forgotten. In fact, I can bet most people only know the name from the video game “Hades.” In contrast to the famous gods, Sisyphus has been washed away by the tides of history. Maybe I will burn up before I reach the sky. Maybe I’ll succeed in sharing my creative outlet with the world. At the end of the day, this is a passion project. And if you made it all the way here, from the bottom of my heart, thank you. Here’s to a sweet 16.

### Q1

I don’t have an ounce of Italian blood. Yet, so much of who I am is Italian.

Growing up, no one was around to take care of me. Two sisters next door, both in their 80s, offered to take me in, watching me until my parents got home from work. At the time it didn’t even register that we were any different; I ate rice, they ate pasta—that was the extent to which we differed. Yet, I’m grateful we did. 

At the dinner table I was taught to observe the harmony, maintaining the cohesion between the family. At lunch I was free to argue, anything else and I was asked “what’s wrong?” In so many ways, I was like their own grandson; whenever I screwed up, they were the first ones to call me out on it. At the time, I hated it. Years later, I wish I had someone there again, willing to do it all over again. That honesty, that love—what I wouldn’t do to have it back.

La dolce vita, the sweet life. More than the name of a famous movie, it’s the ethos of my years there. It’s the little things from them that I’ve picked up—those mean the most to me. The way I move my hands frantically whenever I’m trying to talk, the drawing of shapes with my hands—it’s uniquely Italian. My ability to maintain composure under pressure comes from years of them grilling me for every detail of my life week after week; it was the scariest thing as a kid. I can cook pasta better than fried rice, I live for coffee chats, and the list goes on. 

Community to me isn’t where you were born, it’s what defines you. The little things go a long way, the small steps walk a mile. We never shared the same last name but what I learnt—the honesty, the openness, and the way to cook pasta—remain mine. At Cornell, I’ll bring that same attitude to the classroom. I’ll be that bridge between backgrounds, an intersection of identities. I’ll build a community that feels like family.

### Q2

Whenever I play Civilization VI, I quit before I can win. I never even get close. I just play the beginning over and over, ad infinitum. On the surface, a massive empire seems like the point. Yet, when the size swells, only problems emerge. Cities lack amenities, and the people are miserable; some just outright revolt. None of that shows up unless you look into it, unless you dig past the gold gains, productivity, and sweet, sweet growth. Intangibles exist, and ultimately, they’re what really matter.

Over the summer, my film program explored community. We told our story through struggling bookstores; one person stood out in particular: Janifer. The owner of Sister’s Uptown Bookshop in Harlem, she told us about the nationwide decline in Black-owned bookstores. The community was barely keeping her afloat; the business was never about money. She stayed in that store despite the declining market, regardless of whatever opportunity cost might say. To her it was a space for everyone, one to share the stories of America’s Black voices. She was a cornerstone in her community, yet you wouldn’t know unless you bothered to ask. She has had so much impact, yet there’s nothing to recognize it.

Senior year, I took her story to heart. When our club funding requests came in, things looked dire. As the oldest, it was my job to figure it out. Our budget was slashed by five thousand; the total requested ballooned to six hundred thousand. Even after initial deductions, the ten biggest clubs ate up over half our budget. There were still a hundred and ninety left with pennies to the dollar. I was presented with two opinions: a universal, flat deduction or an intensive tax bracket. Lost, I talked to people. First it was the Model UNs, next the volleyball clubs. Finally, we got to the runts of the pack. Everyone needed the money equally; I had to quantify each club’s value. The Science Olympiad won competitions every year; the debate team had countless trophies. Yet, it was the clubs with ten people that stuck out. Even as a complete outsider, I was offered a soda, given a controller, and sat down to play a game of Super Smash Bros. There was something that didn’t show up in those allocation sheets, something that only those kids knew existed: that community. After talking it over with my team, I made the call: we would institute the bracket. When I made the choice in Civilization, it was never a hard call: prioritize the most efficient gains. Yet, I couldn’t make that same decision. 

Whether I'm wearing pajamas or a suit, whether I’m at my computer or a conference table, I can see those intangibles. Starting corporate finance this year, formally named finance & investments, I’ve learned to apply the same philosophy to the spreadsheets. Every balance sheet I’ve had never tells me the whole story; every single company looks healthy on paper. Yet, almost all of them are on the brink of collapse. An end-of-year report doesn’t tell you about seasonal cycles; a fat profit can’t tell you how bad the debt is. At Cornell, I want to bring my ability to look at a bigger picture, to scan the minutiae, to a whole new community. Through Anabel’s Grocery, Dyson’s AEM 3385: Social Entrepreneurship Practicum, I want to learn firsthand what it means to run a business. Using a Dyson education, I’ll become a thinker, a businessman, who can look past the sheets and at the people; it’s not just the numbers on a sheet that matter.

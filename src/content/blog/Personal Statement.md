Our first-ever conversation was about living life despite knowing death was inevitable—how does one find meaning in a meaningless existence? The question has kept me up since grade school; I was desperate for an answer. I never got one. He just told me to take life as it comes, day by day. It would be a few years until the next time we talked. I didn’t even know he ran a nonprofit at the time—the last thing I saw myself doing was community service work, let alone social work—and I would never have imagined working for him. Yet, for one reason or another, I made that choice. 

When he asked me to work for Family Harmony, long after I had forgotten that first conversation, weeding was the last thing on my mind. I was handed a trash picker and a bag, then sent into the overgrown shrubbery to work—the whole time I had shorts on, exposed to the poison ivy. While building up my bottle cap and soda can collection, keeping my head on a swivel for rats and raccoons, I started to ask why. Why was I spending my Saturday in Chinatown at some park the government didn’t even care enough about to maintain—would any of it make a difference? I could never understand how he did it, how Chris, my boss and mentor, could dedicate his life to such a doomed, meaningless endeavor. As I navigated the three-leaved landmines, it hit me: my being there didn’t matter.

I kept cleaning parks for a while after that—as an excuse to just go outside, really—and eventually I was asked to do a workshop on teen mental health. I went in fully expecting everyone there to actually care, to genuinely try and walk a mile in another’s shoes—after all, they bothered to show up, right? It was like preaching to a choir, only they sang a whole other song. Those parents wanted to learn how to discipline their children, to make them listen, and I was but another measure for these kids to live up to, another metric on an infinite scale. Just like those weeds, it was hopeless—why bother? I had all but given up, succumbing to the suffocating futility, until one mom asked a simple question: “Where can I start?” I had no way of knowing she would change, no way of knowing she cared, but in that moment only one thing mattered: the chance. If I could just make one kid’s life a little better, that would give me purpose.

Thinking back to that first-ever conversation we had, his words finally made sense. I was defeated, overcome with hopelessness, but that mom gave me a glimpse into the answer I’d long been searching for: the meaning was never what I sought; it was a purpose. His advice—to take life as it came, day by day—was never meant to answer that question; I was never meant to fill the void. Rather, it was an effort to help me search for meaning where there was none, for me to find solace in the choice. I was never looking for a reason to live; I was looking for a reason to care. Even if it was just that one person, even if it was ultimately inconsequential, I found my why. 

No matter how pyrrhic the victory, it didn’t really matter. Chasing that fantasy, that unobtainable dream, is enough. That park from years ago is now teeming with people—we played little part in that. We weren’t the ones that restored the park; we were merely the first step in that chain, but the fact that we cared enough to try when no one else would—that’s what mattered. That choice to act despite the apparent futility, even in the face of overwhelming apathy: that is meaning. Even if it’s messy, even if it’s hard, I’ll always make that choice.

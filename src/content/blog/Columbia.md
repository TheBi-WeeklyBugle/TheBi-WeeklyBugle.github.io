### Q2

I've never gone to bed hungry. My parents barely had enough to survive as kids. Eating meat to me is a matter of ethics; to them it’s of food. We’re fundamentally different; there’s no hope in reconciling the difference. Yet, we coexist. 

Every night, I feel like a stranger in my own home. The word “vegan” remains foreign; any attempt at an explanation has only resulted in dismissive, judgmental stares. Despite my best efforts, they won’t budge; meat is pushed onto the plate, eyes locked onto mine. To maintain the fragile stability, I oblige. Whenever they’re not looking, the meat goes back.

Backgrounds anchor perspectives in place; some differences are irreconcilable. I’m willing to compromise, I’m willing to understand, but I won’t be silenced. At Columbia, I won’t be afraid to be loud. To challenge the status quo, to fight for those otherwise unheard—that is what I bring.

### Q3

I’m not a music person, yet I own nearly every Taylor Swift album. When “The Life of a Showgirl” first came out, a friend and I listened through it together; over its 40-minute run time, he lodged nearly 40 complaints. He complained non-stop about the sound; I wouldn’t drop the storytelling. Taylor Swift, to me, is a writer; to my friend, the songs were art, the melodies themselves stories told.

We stopped trying to convince one another. Instead, we took turns listening to it again: one track for the story, the next for the sound. Lost, I asked for directions. He showed me through his playlist, and I gave every artist a shot, learning to listen. Later, I talked him through the lyrics.

Neither of our opinions changed, yet our disagreement resolved. Engagement isn’t about winning; it’s about learning how someone hears the world.

### Q4

My whole life has been spent with an underbite, lollipops sticking perpendicularly out of my mouth. Even when told I look like an anglerfish, I never took it to heart. I can’t dictate reality, but I can control my response. 

Riding home from the orthodontist, though, that’s when I cried. Through no fault of my own, I was condemned to a fate I could only bear. On one particularly rainy drive home, I stared at the sky—there wasn’t any use in shouting at clouds. 

Over the years, I’ve started to embrace it. I bet on myself time and again, not because I'll succeed, but because I can decide whether I fail. Refusing to leave any stone left unturned, I even learnt to write left-handed; there’s so much I can’t control, you might as well focus on what I can. After all, anglerfish are pretty cool.	

### Q5

The curiosity that compels me to question our reality at lunch, the insatiable hunger that keeps me debating between bites—that’s what keeps me asking why.

I’m not interested in just pricing bonds; I want to ask the things that matter. The nature of the human condition and the ethics of a free market are questions uniquely explored in Contemporary Civilization. An education that grounds the quantitative field of economics in the great works of the past—that’s something only the Core could offer.

Outside the classroom, I’m excited to join The Gadfly. I admire the magazine’s non-traditional approach to philosophy and relate to it deeply, having started a philosophy club myself. The focus on non-professional, raw writing is admirable; it adds a necessary authenticity.

At Columbia, I hope to bring those lunchroom debates to a new stage, applying my "why" to a whole new set of questions.

### Q6

Gazing through the train’s window after a long day, letting my mind wander—there’s no greater feeling in the world. A million questions race through my mind; I can’t answer any. 

The ability to satiate my chronic curiosity and the humbling realization that I know nothing—it’s something only philosophy can offer. Simply asking those questions, no matter how impossible they may be, is enough to fill a man’s heart. Economics is but a philosophy on markets, one concerning the everyman. Its ability to predict outcomes and to make those difficult decisions confidently is one without parallel. 

Columbia’s Philosophy & Economics offers an approach seldom found elsewhere, all with a quantitative twist. Professor Lisa Ho’s Economic Development (ECON-4321) particularly drew my interest, its unique mesh of the two perfectly representing the major’s interdisciplinary focus. Either alone can give you half the picture; together they bridge what is and what ought to be.

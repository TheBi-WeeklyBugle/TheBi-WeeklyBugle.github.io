Groundhogs. Every February 2nd, the whole nation watches them come out of their holes. As tradition goes, a groundhog seeing its shadow spells six more weeks of winter. Year after year, almost like clockwork, these little things pop out of their beds, expected to predict the next month and a half’s weather. Astoundingly, even the most famous groundhogs are wrong over half the time; to put that into perspective, guessing would be more successful. 

“This year will be different.” More than the mantra of the New York Jets, it’s what we tell ourselves every year. Whether it’s our New Year’s or the groundhog’s, we expect every wrong to magically right itself. That same delusion that skyrockets Planet Fitness memberships—that’s why we watch groundhogs every February. Deep down we know it’ll never work out, but can you fault a groundhog for dreaming, for trying?

Despite the futility, there’s a joy to be found in that suffering. A groundhog will never become cognizant of the eternal cycle it finds itself in; every year, just like the countless generations of groundhogs before him, he will rise out of that hole and attempt the impossible; blind to his own incompetence, he will try. Yet, in his eventual failure, he finds fulfillment. To live a life destined to predict the future—that is his meaning. Comfortable in his life’s purpose, he can’t help but wonder: is there more out there in the world?

To live a life forever looking at the future, are there fates worse than death? Fulfilling the burden placed upon him at birth, should the groundhog be content with his fate? Is the purpose of life not finding meaning? What more could one ask for? To have the world placed on their shoulders when they’re really just coming out to get laid—is the life of a groundhog not one of lies? Is wanting to sleep in for 6 more weeks just asking too much?

One must imagine the groundhog happy. The eternal quest to predict the weather, the yearly humiliation—is that not enough to fill a groundhog’s heart? That struggle—what would it be without it? The question of the groundhog is a simple, if not strangely philosophical, one; try to stick with me here. It goes: how much wood could a woodchuck chuck if a woodchuck could chuck wood?

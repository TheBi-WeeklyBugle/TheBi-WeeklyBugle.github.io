### Q1

I’m not a music person; my Spotify can never even create a Wrapped. Yet, I own nearly all of Taylor Swift’s physical albums. When “The Life of a Showgirl” first came out, I listened through it with a friend; over its 40-minute run time, he lodged nearly 40 complaints. We spent the next hour analyzing Genius pages like they were holy scripture, only for him to say, “We’ll agree to disagree”—he just didn’t like the way it sounded.

Taylor Swift, to me, is a writer; I’ve never seen her as a musician. The music was a mere distraction, white noise in the background; to my friend, the songs were art, the sounds themselves mediums in which stories are told. In reality, I didn’t know the first thing about music, yet I was so quick to judge. Realizing I was lost, I asked for directions. I gave every artist on his playlist a shot, from Keshi to King Von, learning to listen. I still can’t remember what R&B stands for (or why they’re called Blues), but I’m ever closer to understanding, to finally “getting it.”

People can hum the same tune yet be ever so slightly offbeat. We tend to let subjective realities define objective truths; oftentimes we’re really not so different after all. Fixated on the little things, we lose sight of the larger picture. That day, Taylor Swift showed me more than what it means to be a “Showgirl”; she taught me to say “Tell Me Why.”
